Uge 40¶

The recursive formula for computing convergents¶

Recall from last week that we attempted to compute the \(n\)-th convergent

\[a_0 + \cfrac{1}{a_1 + \cfrac{1}{a_2 + \cfrac{1}{\cdots + \cfrac{1}{a_n}}}}\]

of a continued fraction as a fraction \(\frac{r_n}{s_n}\) by finding formulas for the numerator \(r_n\) and the denominator \(s_n\). Of course, we know the beginning i.e.,

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} r_0 &= a_0\\ s_0 &= 1\\ &\\ r_1 &= a_1 a_0 + 1\\ s_1 &= a_1\\ &\\ r_2 &= a_0 a_1 a_2 + a_0 + a_2\\ s_2 &= a_2 a_1 + 1\\ &\\ &\vdots \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Let us begin this week just by proving the recursive formulas

\[\begin{split}r_n &= a_n r_{n-1} + r_{n-2}\\ s_n &= a_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-2}\end{split}\]

by induction without any mention of Eulers observation. Let us assume that the formulas above hold up to the \(n\)-th convergent and try to prove similar formulas for the \((n+1)\)-st convergent:

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} &a_0 + \cfrac{1}{a_1 + \cfrac{1}{a_2 + \cfrac{1}{\cdots + a_{n-1} + \cfrac{1}{a_n + \cfrac{1}{a_{n+1}}}}}} = \cfrac{\left(a_n + \cfrac{1}{a_{n+1}}\right) r_{n-1} + r_{n-2}}{\left(a_n + \cfrac{1}{a_{n+1}}\right) s_{n-1} + s_{n-2}}\\ &= \cfrac{a_{n+1} a_n r_{n-1} + r_{n-1} + a_{n+1}r_{n-2}}{a_{n+1} a_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-1} + a_{n+1}s_{n-2}} = \cfrac{a_{n+1}(a_n r_{n-1} + r_{n-2}) + r_{n-1}}{a_{n+1}(a_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-2}) + s_{n-1}}\\ &= \cfrac{a_{n+1} r_n + r_{n-1}}{a_{n+1} s_n + s_{n-1}} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

An important observation is that the sequence \(s_0, s_1, s_2, \dots\) of denominators are natural numbers satisfying

(1)\[1 = s_0 \leq s_1 < s_2 < s_3 < \cdots\]

The convergents are in lowest terms¶

A surprising identity shows up for the numerators and denominators in a continued fraction.

(2)\[r_{n+1} s_n - r_n s_{n+1} = (-1)^n\]

for \(n\geq 0\). You can check this identity explicitly for \(n=0\). To prove it in general, we again need to use induction:

\[\begin{split}r_{n+2} s_{n+1} - r_{n+1} s_{n+2} &= (a_{n+2} r_{n+1} + r_n) s_{n+1} - r_{n+1}(a_{n+2} s_{n+1} + s_n) \\ &= r_n s_{n+1} - s_n r_{n+1} = - (r_{n+1} s_n - r_n s_{n+1}) = (-1)^{n+1}.\end{split}\]

If \(d\) is a common divisor of \(r_n\) and \(s_n\), say \(r_n = a d\) and \(s_n = b d\), then

\[r_{n+1}(a d) - (b d) s_{n+1} = d( r_{n+1} a - b s_{n+1}) = (-1)^n\]

by (2). Therefore \(d\) must divide \(1\) and the convergents expressed as \(\frac{r_n}{s_n}\) are in lowest terms.

Even and odd convergents¶

Using (2) it follows that

(3)\[\frac{r_{n+1}}{s_{n+1}} - \frac{r_n}{s_n} = \frac{(-1)^n}{s_n s_{+1}}.\]

Exercise

Prove that

(4)\[\frac{r_{n+2}}{s_{n+2}} - \frac{r_n}{s_n} = (-1)^n\left( \frac{1}{s_n s_{n+1}} - \frac{1}{s_{n+1} s_{n+2}}\right)\]

The identity (4) along with (1) show that

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} &\frac{r_0}{s_0} < \frac{r_2}{s_2} < \frac{r_4}{s_4} < \cdots < \frac{r_{2n}}{s_{2n}} < \cdots\\ &\\ &\frac{r_1}{s_1} > \frac{r_3}{s_3} > \frac{r_5}{s_5} > \cdots > \frac{r_{2n+1}}{s_{2n+1}} > \cdots \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Also, using (3) it follows that

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \frac{r_{2n}}{s_{2n}} &< \frac{r_1}{s_1} \\ &\\ \frac{r_{2n+1}}{s_{2n+1}} &> \frac{r_0}{s_0} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Therefore the sequence \((\frac{r_{2n}}{s_{2n}})\) of even convergents is an increasing sequence of numbers bounded above. Similary the sequence \((\frac{r_{2n +1}}{s_{2n + 1}})\) of odd convergents is a decreasing sequence of numbers bounded below. Each of these sequences have a well defined limit in the real numbers

\[\begin{split}\xi_e &= \lim_{n\to \infty} \frac{r_{2n}}{s_{2n}} \\ &=\\ \xi_o &= \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{r_{2n+1}}{s_{2n+1}}\end{split}\]

It follows from (3) that \(\xi_e = \xi_o\). We call this number \(\xi\). Notice that

\[\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{r_n}{s_n} = \xi.\]

Intuition on increasing sequences bounded from above¶

I am (again) cheating a bit here. Let me explain why it is reasonable to suspect that an increasing sequence of numbers bounded from above defines or converges to a number. Suppose that

\[x_1 \leq x_2 \leq x_3 \leq \cdots\]

is an increasing sequence of numbers and there exists a natural number \(M\), such that \(x_n \leq M\) for every \(n\in \mathbb{N}\). The following bisection procedure leads to the limit.

  1. Let \(\delta := M-x_1\) and \(\Delta := \delta\).
  2. Let \(\Delta := \Delta/2\)
  3. If there are no numbers in \((x_n)\) bigger than \(x_1 + \delta\), then let \(\delta := \delta - \Delta\). Otherwise let \(\delta := \delta + \Delta\)
  4. Repeat from 1.

The continued fraction algorithm converges¶

Recall the fundamental step in the continued fraction algorithm for an irrational number \(\xi\):

\[\xi = \lfloor \xi \rfloor + \xi - \lfloor \xi \rfloor = \lfloor \xi \rfloor + \frac{1}{\cfrac{1}{\xi - \lfloor \xi \rfloor}} = \lfloor \xi \rfloor + \frac{1}{\xi_1}.\]

Repeating this step we end up with an identity

\[\xi = a_0 + \cfrac{1}{a_1 + \cfrac{1}{a_2 + \cfrac{1}{+ \cfrac{\ddots}{a_{n-1} + \cfrac{1}{\xi_n}}}}}\]

after \(n\) steps. This leads again to the identity

\[\xi = \frac{\xi_n r_{n-1} + r_{n-2}} {\xi_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-2}}\]

exactly as we did in the recursive formulas for computing the numerator and the denominator of the convergents. Now a miracle happens:

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \left| \xi - \frac{r_{n-1}}{s_{n-1}} \right| &= \left| \frac{\xi_n r_{n-1} + r_{n-2}} {\xi_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-2}}- \frac{r_{n-1}}{s_{n-1}} \right|= \left| \frac{s_{n-1} (\xi_n r_{n-1} + r_{n-2}) - r_{n-1}(\xi_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-2})}{\xi_n s_{n-1}^2 + s_{n-1} s_{n-2}}\right|\\ &= \left|\frac{s_{n-1} r_{n-2} - r_{n-1} s_{n-2}}{\xi_n s_{n-1}^2 + s_{n-1} s_{n-2}}\right|\\ &\leq \frac{1}{\xi_n s_{n-1}^2 + s_{n-1} s_{n-2}}\\ &= \frac{1}{s_{n-1}(\xi_n s_{n-1} + s_{n-2})} < \frac{1}{s_{n-1}^2}, \end{align*}\end{split}\]

since \(\xi_n > 1\). The upshot of this is quite remarkable. The continued fraction algorithm really converges, since the denominators form a strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers (see (1)). If you have a convergent with a large denominator, then it is a good approximation.

Note

Recall that \(\frac{355}{113}\) is a convergent to \(\pi\). The above inequality says that

\[\left| \pi - \frac{355}{113} \right| < \frac{1}{113^2} \approx 0.0007831\]

The actual approximation is much better: the left hand side is \(\approx 0.0000003\).
